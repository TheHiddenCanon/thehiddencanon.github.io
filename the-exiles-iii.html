The Failure of Translation

Every civilization that has encountered the same phenomenon has translated it into the language available to it. Every translation has failed. The phenomenon has not.

The Hidden Canon
Issue II · 2025
~20 min read

There is a problem that every translator knows and no translation solves. It is not a technical problem — not a matter of vocabulary or grammar or equivalent idiom. It is something more fundamental: the recognition that between any two languages there exists a residue of meaning that cannot be carried across. Something that exists in the original, fully and precisely, and arrives at the destination diminished — not because the translator failed, but because the distance between the two languages is, at its deepest level, incommensurable.

The philosopher Ludwig Wittgenstein put it with characteristic precision at the end of his Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus: "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent." He was talking about logic. But the mystics who read him recognized immediately that he had described their problem with more clarity than most theological treatises.

The experience is real. The experience is transformative. And then the moment passes, and the person must speak — must find words, must locate the experience within a framework that others can understand — and something is lost. Not everything. But something essential, something that was present in the original encounter, does not survive the crossing.

This is the problem we have been circling throughout this trilogy without naming it directly. It is time to name it.

I. The Original Signal

Before any translation, there is the experience itself. We know this experience exists because it has been reported, independently, by too many people in too many contexts to be dismissed. We examined the evidence in The Visitor: the consistency of the phenomenology across cultures and centuries, the neurological correlates, the testimony of practitioners from Socrates to the volunteers in Rick Strassman's DMT study. The pattern is real. Something happens to human beings, under certain conditions, that is not adequately described by any of our existing categories.

The experience shares certain features wherever it appears. There is an encounter with what presents itself as an intelligence — something that is not the self, or not only the self. There is a transmission — information, or something that functions like information. There is an aftermath — a permanent change in the receiver, a before and an after that cannot be undone. And there is the problem: the receiver must now explain what happened.

This is where the translation begins. And this is where the failure begins.

II. Babylon — The Theological Translation

The Sumerian and Babylonian civilizations had developed, over millennia, a comprehensive system for receiving, recording, and transmitting encounters with non-human intelligence. The Anunnaki were part of this system — not its cause but its expression. When a Babylonian priest encountered something in a ritual or dream, he had available to him a complete framework for understanding what he had encountered. The experience was translated, immediately and fluently, into the language of divine beings and divine hierarchies.

This was the strength of the Babylonian translation — and its limitation. The theological framework was so complete that it absorbed the experience entirely. The encounter became, immediately, a communication from a deity — identifiable, classifiable. The surplus, the residue, the element that the framework could not quite contain, was suppressed. Not intentionally, but through the natural tendency of any translation to make the source material fit the target language.

What was suppressed, in the Babylonian theological translation, was the question. It was only when the framework broke — when Babylon destroyed Jerusalem — that the question became unavoidable.

III. The Exiles — The Structural Translation

The Kabbalah represents the most elaborate attempt in Western intellectual history to build a framework adequate to the experience of the Visitor. The Sefer Yetzirah, the Zohar, the Tree of Life: these were the products of centuries of rigorous, sustained, disciplined attention to the structure of the encounter. The Kabbalists were, in the deepest sense, scientists of the interior — empiricists of the non-ordinary.

And yet the Kabbalistic translation also failed. Not by suppressing the question, but by elevating the framework. The map became the territory. The Tree of Life, which was a tool for navigating the encounter, became an object of study in its own right — while the encounter it was designed to navigate receded into the background.

This is the second failure mode of translation: not suppression but substitution. The translation becomes so rich, so internally complex, so intellectually satisfying, that it is mistaken for the original. The Kabbalistic tradition, at its best, preserved awareness of this danger. The warning of the four who entered the Pardes is precisely the tradition's acknowledgment that the map is not the territory — and that mistaking one for the other is genuinely dangerous.

IV. Modernity — The Literal Translation

The modern world has produced a new translation failure, more extreme and more revealing than either of its predecessors. When the theological frameworks of the pre-modern world ceased to function as live explanatory systems, the experience did not stop happening. The Visitor did not cease to arrive.

What happened instead was a crisis of translation. The experience arrived. There was no longer an adequate framework within which to locate it. And so people reached for the frameworks that modernity had produced: the language of science and technology, of empirical investigation, of physical causation. The encounter with the Visitor was translated into the most extreme form available within the materialist-technological framework: extraterrestrial beings, spacecraft, advanced technology, genetic engineering.

Zecharia Sitchin's Anunnaki are not what the Sumerians described. But they are a symptom of something real: the desperate need, in a culture that has lost its theological frameworks, to find some way of saying what the Visitor is. The ancient astronaut theory is a bad translation. But it is a translation of something genuine. And the failure to produce a better one is not Sitchin's failure alone. It is the failure of modernity to develop frameworks adequate to experiences that modernity has not managed to eliminate simply by declaring them impossible.

V. What Survives Every Translation

Here is what we know, after tracing the failure of translation across three thousand years of attempts.

The Babylonian theological translation failed to carry the question. The Kabbalistic structural translation failed to carry the experience. The modern literal translation failed to carry the meaning. Each translation preserved something and lost something. And the phenomenon, across all of them, remained.

What survives every translation is not the framework. It is not the vocabulary. What survives is the bare fact of the encounter — the irreducible datum that something happened, that it was real, that it changed the person it happened to, and that it cannot be fully said.

Wittgenstein was right: whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent. But the tradition's response to this silence has never been simple silence. It has been the endless, frustrated, magnificent attempt to speak anyway — to find words for what exceeds words, to build maps of territories that dissolve any map that touches them, to transmit the untransmittable across the gap between one mind and another, one century and another, one civilization and another.

VI. From the Apkallu to Aiwass

We end where this trilogy began — not with an answer, but with the question made sharper.

The Apkallu rose from the waters. Aiwass spoke from the corner of a room in Cairo. Between them: three thousand years of human beings attempting, with everything available to them, to say what they encountered. Gods. Angels. Emanations. Entities. Aliens. The Holy Guardian Angel. The Self. The subliminal mind. Names for the same experience, none of them adequate, all of them pointing in the same direction.

The Anunnaki are part of this history — not as extraterrestrials, not as the literal gold-mining aliens of Sitchin's imagination, but as one of the earliest recorded attempts to give stable form to something that resists stable form. The Babylonian priests who first systematized them were doing exactly what the Kabbalists would do two millennia later, and what the Golden Dawn would do two millennia after that: building a framework adequate to an experience that exceeded every framework built for it.

Every framework has failed. The experience has not.

The transmission is unbroken. It has been continuous across every civilization that has left records of it — continuous through the Babylonian captivity and the composition of the Zohar and the founding of the Golden Dawn and the Cairo Working of 1904 and the clinical trials in Albuquerque.

The language between the Apkallu and Aiwass has failed, every time, to carry what it was asked to carry.

The transmission itself has never failed.
