What the Persecution Reveals

The Paulicians were moved to Bulgaria to neutralize them. What happened next is the key to the entire trilogy — and to the three-thousand-year pattern we have been tracing. The transmission did not survive despite the persecution. It survived because of it.

The Hidden Canon
Issue IV · 2025
~24 min read

There is a detail in the history of the Paulicians that the Byzantine Empress Theodora, who ordered the massacre of perhaps a hundred thousand of them in 843 CE, would have found deeply unwelcome had she lived to understand it.

She did not destroy them. She planted them.

To understand why, we need to begin at the beginning of the chain — not with the Gnostics of Egypt, not with the Cathars of southern France, but with a man named Constantine-Silvanus in a village called Mananalis in eastern Anatolia, sometime around 650 CE, who received a gift of a New Testament from a deacon traveling through, and read it, and concluded that the Christianity he found there bore almost no resemblance to the Christianity the institutional Church was teaching.

I. The Paulicians

Constantine-Silvanus took his name from the apostle Paul and Paul's companion Silvanus, and the movement he founded called itself Paulician — followers of Paul, of the direct apostolic transmission, before the institution had intervened.

Their theology was, in its structural bones, the same theology we have been tracing since the first essay of this trilogy. The material world was the creation of a lesser or malevolent principle — the God of the Old Testament, in their reading, was not the Father of Jesus but an inferior deity, a craftsman of a prison. The true God was the God of the New Testament: the Father revealed by Christ, entirely spiritual, entirely good, having nothing to do with the cruelty and suffering built into the fabric of physical existence. They rejected the Old Testament. They rejected the institutional Church and all its apparatus — the sacraments, the priesthood, the veneration of Mary, the cross (which they found obscene: why worship the instrument of an execution?), the icons that filled Byzantine churches.

What they kept was direct. They read Paul. They gathered in communities called synaxeis — assemblies. They passed their transmission person to person, teacher to student, in the manner of the earliest Christians. They had no professional clergy, no liturgical apparatus, no buildings consecrated to sacred purpose. The encounter, in their understanding, required none of these things.

The Byzantine Empire disagreed, forcefully and repeatedly. Several emperors launched campaigns of persecution. Constantine V deported communities of Paulicians to Thrace in the eighth century — the first forced migration, intended to break up concentrations of heretics and scatter them among Orthodox populations where they would be diluted and disappear.

They did not disappear.

The Paulicians who were moved to the Balkans kept their communities, kept their transmission, kept their reading of Paul. And in the ninth century, pushed out of Anatolia entirely, a significant portion of the remaining Paulician community built a fortified city called Tephrike, in what is now eastern Turkey, which became the capital of a briefly independent Paulician state.

Tephrike fell in 872 CE to the Emperor Basil I. The Paulician leadership was killed. The community was, once again, scattered — some fleeing east into Arab territory, some absorbed into the Byzantine population, some moving west into the Balkans, into the communities of Paulician descendants that had already been planted there a century earlier by Constantine V.

This is the mechanism. The Empire kept trying to solve the Paulician problem by moving Paulicians. It moved them to Bulgaria and Thrace. Then it destroyed Tephrike and scattered the survivors. And in the soil it had spent a century preparing — the soil of the Balkans, already seeded with Paulician communities — something new began to grow.

II. The Bogomils and the Prepared Ground

Around 950 CE, in Bulgaria, a priest named Bogomil — the name means dear to God — began to teach.

We do not have his own words. What we have is a document written against him by a Bulgarian Orthodox priest named Cosmas, sometime around 972 CE: the Sermon Against the Heretics, which is our earliest detailed account of what Bogomil taught. Cosmas wrote to refute and condemn — but adversarial accounts, as we noted with the Gnostics and the Church Fathers, often preserve more than their authors intended.

What Cosmas describes is structurally identical to what the Paulicians had taught, with elements of Gnostic dualism woven through. The visible world is the creation of a Satanael — a diminished divine being, not co-equal with God. The God of the Old Testament is this lesser creator. Christ came from the true God to reveal the reality beyond the prison of matter. The institutional Church — its sacraments, its priesthood, its icons, its cross — is the machinery of the false world, not a path to liberation but an obstacle to it. Salvation comes through direct knowledge of the spiritual reality, transmitted person to person, through prayer and reading and communal life stripped of material attachment.

The organizational structure mirrored the Paulician model: an inner circle of the perfecti, who had received the initiatory rite administered by the laying on of hands, and an outer circle of ordinary believers.

The question of whether Bogomil himself had direct contact with Paulician communities in Bulgaria cannot be answered with certainty. What can be said is that he taught in a country that had been seeded with Paulician communities for a century, and that what he taught was, structurally, the Paulician teaching. The soil was prepared. The seed was there.

III. The Chain Made Visible

The Bogomil movement spread — through Bulgaria, through the Byzantine Empire, through Serbia, through Bosnia, and west. By the eleventh century, communities with recognizably Bogomil-adjacent beliefs were appearing in northern Italy. These Italian communities, called Patarenes or Cathari, maintained active contact with Bogomil churches in the Balkans and Constantinople. They were not rediscoveries. They were branches of a living transmission.

And then, in 1167 CE, the chain assembled in one room.

At a council held at Saint-Félix-de-Caraman, near Toulouse in southern France, the major Cathar communities of western Europe gathered. Presiding over the council was a man named Nicetas — a Bogomil bishop from Constantinople. He had traveled west specifically to bring the Cathar churches into proper organizational relationship with the eastern tradition. He brought with him the consolamentum as his church practiced it. He reorganized the Cathar bishoprics. He connected the French communities directly and formally to the chain that ran east through Italy to the Balkans to the Bogomil church to — if you follow it back far enough — the Paulician communities planted in Bulgarian soil by Byzantine emperors who had been trying to destroy them.

The chain from the eastern Anatolian highlands to the fields of southern France is now fully documented, with names and dates at each link. It spans three centuries and crossed the width of the known world. And the single most important thing to understand about it is this:

"At every link in the chain, it was the attempt to destroy the transmission that carried it forward. The institution kept trying to solve the problem by destroying the communities. It kept discovering that destroying a community disperses its members — and dispersed members carry their knowledge with them into new soil."

— The Hidden Canon

IV. Suppression as the Mechanism

Persecution is not the enemy of this transmission. It is, again and again, the mechanism of its spread.

This is not a romantic paradox. It is a structural fact with a mundane explanation. When a community is suppressed, it does not cease to exist. Its members scatter. And scattered members, carrying their knowledge into new territories, into new cultural soil, encounter new populations who have been asking the same questions and reaching the same conclusions independently — and when the transmission and the independent inquiry meet, the result is a new community, in a new place, that the institution will then need to suppress.

The Byzantine Empire spent two centuries suppressing the Paulicians. It created the Bogomils. The Byzantine Church suppressed the Bogomils. They spread to Italy and France. The Papacy crusaded against the Cathars for twenty years. Survivors reached Bosnia, where the Bosnian Church — the Crkva Bosanska — lasted two more centuries, until the Ottoman conquest of 1463. At each step, the suppression was both effective as a local matter and catastrophic as a global one: effective in destroying this particular community, catastrophic in spreading the transmission it was trying to contain.

This pattern is not unique to this tradition. We saw it in The Exiles: the Babylonian captivity produced, in captivity, the most intellectually fertile period in Jewish religious history. Exile and suppression and forced displacement turned monolatry into monotheism, produced Ezekiel's vision and the Merkabah tradition, created the conditions for the Kabbalah. The institution of imperial power, trying to neutralize a transmission, placed its carriers in direct contact with the most sophisticated magical tradition in the ancient world — and what came back from that captivity was more powerful than anything that had gone in.

The pattern holds across three thousand years: every attempt to suppress the transmission creates the conditions for its next emergence. The method cannot be buried because the people who carry it, when scattered, carry it everywhere.

V. What This Reveals About the Transmission

There is a last question to ask, and it is the hardest one.

Why does it keep returning to the same structural conclusions?

Wherever the transmission appears — Gnostic Egypt, Paulician Anatolia, Bogomil Bulgaria, Cathar France, Merkabah Jerusalem, the Kabbalists of medieval Spain, the practitioners we traced in The Architects — it arrives, independently or through transmission, at the same set of recognitions: that the world as it presents itself is not the whole of reality; that there is something beyond the boundary of ordinary experience; that direct contact with that something is possible; that such contact requires preparation, discipline, and a specific kind of knowing that is not ordinary intellectual knowledge; that the institutions built to manage this contact inevitably replace the contact with authority over the contact; and that the transmission is, in consequence, always in tension with the institutions that attempt to contain it.

We hold two readings open simultaneously. The first is the skeptical one: these ideas recur because they are the natural conclusions of serious inquiry applied to the human condition — the world is full of suffering and injustice, and any honest person who looks at this long enough will eventually ask whether there is something the ordinary picture is missing. The second is the more radical one: these ideas recur because they are tracking something real — because the encounter with the Visitor is a genuine phenomenon, and independent investigators in different centuries keep finding it and reporting it in structurally similar terms because they are encountering the same thing.

We cannot adjudicate between these readings. That is not evasion: it is the epistemically honest position, and the entire Hidden Canon has been built on the refusal to resolve a question that the available evidence cannot resolve. But the Paulician-Bogomil-Cathar chain gives us something the earlier essays could not: a documented case in which both readings are simultaneously true. The structural ideas recur independently and are transmitted historically. And at every point, the attempt to end the recurrence only accelerates the transmission.

The Empress Theodora killed a hundred thousand Paulicians. And the Paulician communities she moved to Bulgaria, before the massacre, are the most likely seed of the Bogomil movement that seeded the Cathar movement that produced some of the most serious practitioners of direct inner knowledge that medieval Europe ever saw. She could not have done more for the transmission if she had been trying to spread it.

On the Shape of the Question

We end this trilogy where we began: with a jar buried in the desert, and what it contained.

The monk who buried the Nag Hammadi texts in 367 CE was not trying to transmit them. He was trying to save them — to get them out of reach of the men who wanted to burn them. He did not know that what he was doing was ensuring that what he buried would be found, sixteen centuries later, by a farmer who nearly used it to start a fire, and would end up in the hands of scholars who would recognize in it a tradition that the institution had believed it had entirely destroyed.

The Paulicians, the Bogomils, the Cathars — each of them, at the moment of suppression, became seeds. Not because they intended to. Not because there was a hidden network or a secret school coordinating their survival. But because knowledge, when driven underground, moves laterally through the substrate of human inquiry and surfaces where the conditions are right.

This is what the persecution reveals: not that the tradition is indestructible in some mystical sense, but that the ideas it carries are recurrent in a way that no institutional force has yet found a method to permanently suppress. Every century, in new soil, with new vocabulary, the same recognition arrives: that the world as the institution describes it is not the whole of reality, that direct contact with what lies beyond it is possible, and that the institution's authority over that contact is contingent, not necessary.

"The jar keeps being buried. The jar keeps being found."

— The Hidden Canon
